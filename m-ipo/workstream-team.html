E Workstream E · Commercial readiness · Tier two

Team and capability.

Marketing operations leadership, succession, public-company readiness. The honest question of whether the team that built marketing can take it through scrutiny, and survive life as a listed company. The most personal of the five, and the one most often left too late.

What it tests Whether the team can scale to public life
The risk Key-person dependency
The real test Could the function survive a departure
What good looks like

A team that can be looked at.

The gold standard is a function that does not depend on any one person staying, led by someone who owns marketing operations as a discipline, with senior people who can hold their own in front of investors.

Operations leadership

Owned, not borrowed

Someone owns marketing operations as a real discipline, with the authority to set standards, rather than it being a side-of-desk task for an overloaded generalist.

No single point of failure

Knowledge written down

The function survives a key departure because the methods and decisions are documented, not carried in one indispensable head.

Investor-ready people

Without a chaperone

Senior marketers who can answer hard questions credibly in investor and adviser interactions, rather than a CMO who has to carry every room alone.

Spencer Stuart puts average CMO tenure at 4.3 years. A listing tests whether your function quietly depends on any one person staying.

What has to be true

Four conditions the team has to meet.

This is the workstream that cannot be fixed in the final quarter. These are the conditions that take the longest to reach, which is exactly why they have to be faced early.

01

Operations leadership exists

A named owner for marketing operations, with the remit and the authority to set and hold standards across the function.

02

Succession is documented

Key-person risk identified and reduced, with the knowledge that matters written down and cover that has actually been tested.

03

The team is investor-ready

Senior marketers who can answer hard questions from investors and advisers credibly, prepared rather than hoping to improvise.

04

Capability matches the ambition

The skills the equity story promises are skills the team actually has, not ones the narrative quietly assumes into existence.

What has to exist

What answers can this team do it.

The team workstream produces uncomfortable documents, because honest ones are the only useful kind. By the time the function is under scrutiny, these have to exist.

01

Capability assessment

An honest read of the function against what a listed company actually needs, naming gaps rather than assuming readiness.

02

Org and succession plan

Roles, owners, cover for key people and hiring priorities, so the structure is deliberate rather than inherited.

03

Operations leadership definition

The role, its remit and who holds it, so marketing operations has an owner and not just a vague collective responsibility.

04

Knowledge documentation

The methods and decisions that currently live in people’s heads, written down so the function does not walk out of the door with them.

05

Investor-readiness brief

Who fronts what, prepared and rehearsed, so investor interactions are handled rather than survived.

06

Capability development plan

The route from today’s team to the public-company team, with dates rather than good intentions.

Evidence quantum

Readiness is proven by removing a person.

The honest test of this workstream is whether the function would survive losing someone important. Every claim about the team has to stand up to that question, not to an org chart.

What marketing says “We have the team for this.”
What has to back it A capability assessment that names the gaps honestly, not an org chart drawn on the assumption that everyone is already ready.
“We are not dependent on one person.”
What has to back it Documented cover for every key role, tested by someone other than the person who normally does it, rather than assumed to exist.
“Our people can face investors.”
What has to back it Named spokespeople who are prepared and rehearsed, not a quiet hope that the CMO can personally carry every difficult room.
“We can grow into it.”
What has to back it A development plan with dates and milestones, not an intention to sort the team out somewhere nearer the listing.
