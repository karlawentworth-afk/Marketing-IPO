Tech and governance.

The martech and AI estate, controlled and auditable access, and lawful data under GDPR. The layer that decides whether everything above it is defensible or just optimistic.

What it carries Every number marketing reports
The quiet killer Data nobody can trace
The real test Can you show where a number came from
What good looks like

Infrastructure you never have to apologise for.

Nobody gets excited about a data pipeline. But the spine is what lets every number above it be trusted, and these are the operating targets a gold-standard function holds itself to.

<5%

MarTech as % of budget

The stack earns its place. Spend much above this and the tools are running you, not the other way round.

1

System of record

One source of truth for the numbers that matter, not a federation of spreadsheets that disagree politely.

5 day

Monthly close

The numbers are ready early enough in the month to be useful, every month, through the same routine.

8 qtr

Lineage retained

You can trace any reported number back two years without launching an archaeology project to find it.

These are operating targets, not laws of physics. The point is holding a standard, not hitting a magic figure.

What has to be true

Four conditions the spine has to meet.

The spine is where the other workstreams either stand up or quietly subside. These are the states it has to be in before any number leaves the building.

01

MarTech under five percent

Every tool earns its place, the architecture is documented, and there are no shadow systems doing important work that nobody officially knows about.

02

Data lineage you can trace

Every reported number walks back to a source system without a leap of faith in the middle. If you cannot show the path, you cannot defend the number.

03

A close that actually closes

Monthly, on a set date, through the same process. Not a quarterly scramble that depends on one person being available and caffeinated.

04

Governance and access documented

Who can change what, who signs off, and a log that shows it happened. Diligence looks hard at who had their hands on the numbers.

What has to exist

What diligence will ask to see.

When the reporting accountants reach the operations workstream, they are not interested in assurances. They want documents. By the time they ask, these have to exist.

01

Stack inventory and architecture map

Every tool, what it does, what it costs, and how data moves between them. The picture that ends the ‘wait, we pay for that?’ conversation.

02

Data lineage documentation

The path each key number takes from where it is captured to where it is reported. Written down, not held in one person’s memory.

03

Monthly close calendar and process

The dated, repeatable routine that produces the numbers, so the close is a process and not an act of heroism.

04

Governance and access register

Roles, permissions, change control and sign-off rights, kept current and matching what the systems actually allow.

05

Vendor and contract schedule

Renewal dates, owners, and data-processing terms for every tool. The list legal will need and marketing usually cannot produce.

06

System reconciliation log

Where systems are checked against each other, when, and what the check found. Proof the numbers are watched, not just generated.

Evidence quantum

The spine is proven in worked examples.

You cannot assert your way through this workstream. Every claim about the infrastructure has to be demonstrable on demand, usually by picking something at random and following it through.

What marketing says “Our stack is under control.”
What has to back it An inventory with cost per tool set against budget, an architecture map showing how data flows, and nothing turning up on the bank statement that is missing from the list.
“The numbers are traceable.”
What has to back it A worked example on demand: pick any reported figure, walk it back to its source on paper, and find no gaps where a number simply appears.
“We close monthly.”
What has to back it Twelve consecutive dated close packs, not three good ones and a promise. Consistency through quiet months and busy ones is the actual claim.
“Access is governed.”
What has to back it A permissions register that matches what the systems really allow, with a change log that someone genuinely keeps rather than reconstructs the night before.
