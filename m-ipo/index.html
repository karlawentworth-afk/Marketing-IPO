Marketing operations · IPO readiness · Transaction preparation

Marketing operations
for serious moments.

Some moments in a business demand more of marketing than business-as-usual. An IPO. A transaction. A turnaround. This is a working guide to what marketing has to get right when the scrutiny is real, built around the way an IPO actually examines a business.

Level one · The whole picture

An IPO is not a marketing project.

It is the most thorough public examination of every part of a business. Eight workstreams run in parallel. Each has standards. Each has scrutiny. Marketing is one of them.

Twelve to twenty-four months before a company lists on the London Stock Exchange, the work begins across eight distinct workstreams. Some can stop the listing on their own. Others determine what investors pay. Knowing which is which is the foundation of everything that follows.

Level two · Where marketing sits

Marketing is tier two, with tier one consequences.

Marketing is rarely the workstream that stops a listing. Finance, legal and governance failures do that. But marketing's operational quality directly determines what the finance team can credibly disclose, and marketing's narrative work determines what investors pay. Both flow back into the things that genuinely matter.

Tier one

Deal-breakers

Gaps here mean the IPO is withdrawn or lists at a heavy discount.

  • Finance and accounting
  • Legal and corporate
  • Governance and board
Tier two, marketing is here

Valuation-determining

Gaps don't stop the listing but knock 20-40% off proceeds. Real money.

  • Internal controls and risk
  • Technology and systems
  • Commercial readiness (includes marketing)
Tier three

Important but recoverable

Material but addressable in the final months if needed.

  • People and remuneration
  • ESG and sustainability
The honest position

Marketing won't stop the IPO. Marketing's operational quality will determine what your finance team can disclose with confidence.

That distinction matters. The CRO and CFO don't need to be sold on marketing's importance in the abstract. They need to understand that marketing's numbers feed finance's audited KPI disclosures. If those numbers don't reconcile, the disclosed KPIs get softer, investor confidence drops, and the multiple suffers. This is where marketing earns its seat.

Level three · What needs to be true

The conditions that have to hold.

Across every successful pre-IPO marketing function, the same conditions are present. These are not features. They are the standards a marketing function meets before it walks into formal scrutiny.

01

One number, one source

Marketing and finance produce the same answer to the same question. CAC, LTV, pipeline, attribution. No parallel sets of numbers.

02

Eight quarters of clean data

Two years minimum of methodologically consistent data. Reporting accountants and bookrunners look at trend reliability.

03

Documented attribution

Methodology writable in two pages. Consistent application. Reconciled with audited financials at month-end close.

04

Defensible unit economics

CAC fully loaded, LTV from real retention curves, payback period within benchmark. Methodology survives challenge.

05

Sales-marketing alignment

Shared definitions in the CRM, not just aligned ones. One pipeline number. Documented service-level agreements.

06

Equity story discipline

Marketing-owned narrative aligned with the financial story and operating reality. Tested before bookrunners arrive.

07

MarTech under five percent

Marketing technology spend under 5% of marketing budget. Architecture documented. Data lineage traceable.

08

Team capable of public life

Marketing operations leadership in place. Succession documented. Senior team ready for investor interactions.

Level five · How the work gets done

Every task answers three questions.

Inside each area, the work breaks down into specific tasks. The methodology behind those tasks is the same in every case. The detailed playbook stays off the page. The framework is here.

Each task in each area is designed to answer three questions. What artefact has to exist by the time bookrunners arrive. What evidence proves it. Who has signed off on it and when. Get those three things right for every task and the marketing function survives any scrutiny it meets.

Question one

What artefact has to exist?

Every task produces something concrete. A methodology document. A reconciled report. A trained team. Not a vague capability. A specific artefact someone could pull out of a drawer.

Question two

What evidence proves it?

Data with documented lineage. Working papers showing the calculation. Sign-off trails. Audit logs. Evidence that survives the bookrunners' challenge, not internal-only assurance.

Question three

Who owns it, and what comes next?

Ownership is a person, not a function. Status is current. Dependencies are visible. The next step is unambiguous. No task drifts because no task is unowned.
