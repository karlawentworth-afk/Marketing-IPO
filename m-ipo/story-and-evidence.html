Story and evidence.

The equity story, market positioning, customer evidence. Every public claim must be provable, not just polished. The words that end up in the prospectus, the analyst presentation and the roadshow deck have to survive scrutiny, and that means one story everywhere, with evidence behind every line of it.

What it becomes The prospectus and roadshow story
Who reads it Bookrunners, analysts, investors
The real test Does the story match the numbers
What good looks like

A story that is compelling and true.

The gold standard here is the hardest balance in the five workstreams: a narrative genuinely worth listening to that the numbers can stand behind every word of. Inspiring is fine. Unsupportable is fatal.

One story

Told everywhere

The equity story, the website, the sales pitch and the numbers all say the same thing. No daylight between the version investors hear and the version customers do.

Real proof

Evidence that checks out

Every customer claim is referenceable and permissioned, every proof point survives a phone call. Bookrunners verify, and they are good at it.

Grounded ambition

A story the numbers carry

The narrative reaches, but never past what workstream A can defend. A story that outruns the numbers does not impress investors, it worries them.

The story is marketing’s to write. The discipline is never letting it travel further than the evidence.

What has to be true

Four conditions the narrative has to meet.

A great story with a weak foundation is worse than no story, because it invites the scrutiny that exposes it. These are the conditions that keep the narrative defensible.

01

Equity story discipline

A single marketing-owned narrative, aligned with the financial story and the operating reality, tested hard before bookrunners ever see it.

02

Positioning that holds everywhere

The same claim on the website, in the sales deck and in the prospectus. Inconsistency across surfaces reads to investors as risk.

03

Customer evidence that is verifiable

References permissioned, case studies factual, logos you are actually allowed to use. Every proof point ready for someone to check it.

04

A story tied to the numbers

Every claim of momentum traces to something workstream A can stand behind. The narrative and the data tell one story, not two.

What has to exist

What goes in front of investors.

The narrative workstream produces the material that ends up in the most scrutinised documents a company ever publishes. By the time the roadshow looms, these have to be built and tested.

01

The equity story document

The core narrative, owned by marketing and signed off by the exec, that everything public is built from.

02

Positioning and messaging framework

The claims, the proof behind each one, and the words, applied consistently across every surface.

03

Customer evidence library

References, case studies and permissions, all current and usable, so proof is on hand rather than scrambled for.

04

Competitive and market positioning map

Where the company sits, argued with sources rather than adjectives, because bookrunners will test the claim.

05

Narrative-to-numbers traceability

Each headline claim linked to the supporting metric, so the story and the data never contradict each other in the room.

06

Public surface audit

Website, decks and collateral checked against the equity story, so nothing public quietly undermines the official version.

Evidence quantum

A story is only as strong as its weakest proof point.

Investors do not take narrative on trust, and bookrunners are paid to find the gap between the claim and the evidence. Every line of the story has to be able to answer for itself.

What marketing says “We have a clear equity story.”
What has to back it A single document the CEO, CFO and CMO all recognise as the story, not three different versions living in three different decks.
“Our customers love us.”
What has to back it Named, permissioned references who will take the call, and case studies whose numbers are real and hold up when someone checks them.
“We lead the market.”
What has to back it A positioning map with sources behind the claim, not a confident adjective. Bookrunners test market-leadership statements specifically.
“The story is consistent.”
What has to back it A surface audit showing the website, the sales deck and the draft prospectus telling the same story, with the discrepancies already fixed.
