The numbers.

CAC, LTV, payback, attribution, marketing-sourced revenue. This is the data layer that feeds finance's disclosed KPIs and has to hold up when the reporting accountants pull on it. It is also where most marketing functions discover their numbers were never built to be looked at this closely.

What it feeds Finance's audited KPI disclosures
Evidence floor Eight quarters of consistent data
The real test Does it reconcile to the audited P&L
What good looks like

Numbers that reconcile, not numbers that impress.

The gold standard here is boring on purpose. Marketing and finance answer the same question the same way, every headline figure traces back to a system someone can open, and the unit economics survive a stranger asking how they were built.

Before an investor pays a multiple, someone independent checks whether your growth is efficient or just expensive. They do it with four or five numbers that have become the shared language of the market. You do not have to hit every benchmark. You do have to know where you sit against each one, and be able to explain it without flinching.

3:1

LTV to CAC

The ratio investors reach for first. Below it and you are buying growth inefficiently. Far above it and you are probably underinvesting.

12-18 mo

CAC payback

The band the market expects a B2B business to recover acquisition cost in. Drift past it and the growth model gets questioned.

40+

Rule of 40

Growth rate plus profit margin. The single figure that frames whether your growth is worth what it costs to buy.

7.7%

Marketing as % of revenue

Gartner's cross-industry average. A sanity check on whether spend is in a normal range, never a target to chase.

The numbers move by sector and stage. The discipline of knowing yours, and defending it, does not.

What has to be true

Four conditions the numbers have to meet.

These are not nice-to-haves. They are the state a marketing function's data has to be in before it walks into formal scrutiny. Miss one and the whole workstream is exposed.

01

One number, one source

Marketing and finance produce the same answer to the same question. CAC, LTV, pipeline, attribution. No second set of numbers living in a deck somewhere.

02

Eight quarters of clean data

Two years minimum, built on one consistent methodology. Reporting accountants and bookrunners are not checking a snapshot. They are checking whether the trend can be trusted.

03

Documented attribution

The method is written down, short enough to read in one sitting, applied the same way every month, and reconciled against the audited financials at close.

04

Defensible unit economics

CAC carries its full cost, LTV comes off real retention curves rather than hope, payback sits within benchmark. The method survives someone competent pushing on it.

What has to exist

Things you could pull out of a drawer.

A capability you can describe is not the same as an artefact someone can inspect. By the time bookrunners arrive, the numbers workstream has to have produced concrete, finished things. These are them.

01

KPI definitions register

Every reported marketing number, defined once, with its owning source system and the calculation behind it. The document that ends the argument about whose figure is right.

02

Unit economics methodology

Fully-loaded CAC, LTV from actual retention, payback period. Written so a reporting accountant can follow every step without a meeting to explain it.

03

Attribution methodology document

The model, its rules, and its honest limits, set out in two pages. Long enough to be real, short enough that nobody can claim it is unknowable.

04

Marketing-sourced revenue reconciliation

Marketing's revenue claim tied back to closed-won in the CRM and to the audited P&L, refreshed at month-end. The bridge between what marketing says it did and what finance can sign.

05

Eight-quarter restated dataset

Two years of history rebuilt onto the single agreed methodology, with every restatement documented and dated. The proof that the trend is real and not redrawn to flatter.

06

Monthly close pack

The working papers and sign-offs that show the numbers were produced through a process, not assembled the night before. The thing that turns a figure into evidence.

Evidence quantum

Evidence is what survives the challenge.

A number existing is not the same as a number being provable. Evidence quantum is the weight and kind of proof each claim has to carry before it is allowed into a disclosure. Here is what each claim actually costs to make.

What marketing says "Our CAC is X."
What has to back it A fully-loaded cost build that includes the cost most people leave out, extracted from the source systems, broken out month by month, and reconciled to the ledger. Not a media-spend figure wearing a CAC label.
"Marketing sourced Y of revenue."
What has to back it Opportunity-level data with documented attribution rules, tied to closed-won in the CRM and through to the audited P&L. The rules have to be the same ones used last quarter and the quarter before.
"Payback is Z months."
What has to back it Cohort-level retention behaviour, not a single blended assumption applied across everyone. The shape of the curve is the argument, and the curve has to come from real data.
"The trend is reliable."
What has to back it Eight quarters built on one methodology, with every restatement logged and dated. If the method changed halfway through, the trend is decoration, and a good adviser will say so.
